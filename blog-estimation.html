Estimation, a piece of cake?

Oct 2018

During the last estimation training day I participated in, I decided I wanted a simple way to explain the complexity of estimation, and to highlight the shared ownership. For context, the activity was to estimate full scope, which often comes up in cases where funding is less agile. In practice, it can be pretty darn risky to mix lean requirements and fixed bid, and here's why.

Recipe for success?

I've been working on this little scenario: estimating the effort to deliver cake.

Let's think about the deliverable - cake. Take a moment to picture it. Cupcakes, bunt, or other? Single or multi-tiered? Simple or ornate? Now, without getting too granular, consider other unknowns: What's the event? Who's the cake for? Are we coordinating with other vendors?

There are endless assumptions that could be made, and yours may differ from the person next to you. At one end it might be cupcakes with icing and sprinkles for a kids birthday. At the other, a multi-tiered cake with hand made decorations for a wedding, plus coordinating with the caterers and venue.

What this highlights, is the risk of the unknown. Do not underestimate the importance of validating assumptions and developing shared understanding. Use agile workshops and discovery, focus on outcomes over features, and engage the whole team early in the process. The bigger the scope, the more assumptions and unknowns, and lean requirements may only scratch the surface.

Estimating what you doughnut know

Let's assume we settled on the ornate wedding cake, what if I asked someone to estimate it - does everyone know how long it takes to bake? Decorate? What about each fondant flower? And working with the client, caterer and venue - are those potential risks you can estimate? Lastly, how confident are you in these estimates?

I should admit now that I don't bake, so I haven't a clue, and I would ask someone who does. However that makes for a different problem, where I may have an accurate estimate, but not for my pace.

This brings me to the second risk, extraneous variables. From subject matter expertise to experience in the act of estimating itself, there are too many variables to control for. You don't know what you don't know till you know, and estimates are best guesses and should be treated as such. Relative estimates and actual velocity are your friends.

Icing on the cake

The last bit I thought I'd touch on, is the positive correlation between uncertainty and padding. Next time you catch yourself layering it on to protect you and your team, take that as helpful hint that you may have too many assumptions or unknowns. If you have a long list, start validating. If that's not possible, consider slicing up your roadmap, with goals to validate certain assumptions (often tech spikes).


Moral of the story, estimation is not a piece of cake
It's been a slice, cheers to the future cake bosses
